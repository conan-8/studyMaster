# AP Courses — Skills & Knowledge Master Breakdown

**Purpose:** A course-by-course reference for building an AP study/question bank. For every AP course, this document lists (1) the exam format, (2) the skills students must be able to *perform*, and (3) the knowledge students must *possess*, at the granularity of College Board Course and Exam Description (CED) units. Current as of the 2025–26 school year, with 2026–27 launches noted.

**How to read the classification:**
- **Skill-dominant** — the exam is a performance; knowing facts cannot compensate for weak execution (e.g., Calculus).
- **Knowledge-dominant** — the exam wraps questions in skills language, but a large required corpus gates everything (e.g., APUSH).
- **Hybrid** — a real corpus *and* a real procedural skill set; students genuinely fail from either side (e.g., Chemistry).

## AP Calculus AB
**Exam format:** 45 MCQ (105 min, 50%) + 6 FRQ (90 min, 50%). Calculator allowed on parts of both sections.

**Skills needed:**
- Execute derivative procedures fluently: power, product, quotient, chain rules; implicit differentiation; derivatives of inverse functions
- Execute integration procedures: antiderivatives, u-substitution, accumulation functions
- Translate contextual scenarios (motion, related rates, optimization) into calculus setups
- Interpret the derivative as rate of change and the integral as accumulation, in words
- Justify conclusions using named theorems: IVT, EVT, MVT, FTC — with conditions verified
- Read and reason from tables and graphs of f, f′, and f″ (function behavior: increasing/decreasing, concavity, extrema, points of inflection)
- Solve separable differential equations; interpret slope fields; exponential models
- Compute areas between curves and volumes of revolution (disk/washer)
- Write mathematically precise justifications in FRQs (the "because..." sentence earns points, not the answer alone)

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–8):**
1. Limits and Continuity — limit definitions, limit laws, continuity, asymptotes, squeeze theorem
2. Differentiation: Definition and Basic Rules — average vs. instantaneous rate, power/exponential/trig rules
3. Differentiation: Composite, Implicit, and Inverse — chain rule, implicit, inverse trig derivatives
4. Contextual Applications — position/velocity/acceleration, related rates, linearization, L'Hôpital's Rule
5. Analytical Applications — MVT/EVT, first/second derivative tests, optimization, curve sketching
6. Integration and Accumulation of Change — Riemann sums, FTC, u-substitution, accumulation functions
7. Differential Equations — slope fields, separation of variables, exponential growth/decay
8. Applications of Integration — area, volume (disk/washer), average value, net change

**Question-bank notes:** Distractors should be generated from procedural errors (dropped chain rule, sign errors, wrong FTC bounds). FRQ scoring must award justification steps, not just final answers. Include table-based and graph-based stems — students must integrate calculus reasoning with data representation.

## AP Calculus BC
**Exam format:** Same structure as AB (45 MCQ + 6 FRQ); roughly 60% of content overlaps AB.

**Skills needed:** All AB skills, plus:
- Integration by parts and partial fractions
- Evaluate improper integrals
- Work parametric, polar, and vector-valued functions (derivatives, arc length, motion)
- Apply convergence tests systematically (choosing the right test is itself the skill)
- Construct and manipulate Taylor/Maclaurin polynomials and power series; error bounds (Lagrange, alternating series)

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–10):** AB Units 1–8, plus:
9. Parametric Equations, Polar Coordinates, and Vector-Valued Functions — parametric derivatives, polar area, vector motion
10. Infinite Sequences and Series — nth-term, integral, comparison, ratio, alternating tests; absolute vs. conditional convergence; radius/interval of convergence; Taylor series and error

**Question-bank notes:** Series items should test *test selection strategy* ("which test applies and why"), not just execution. Parametric/polar items are chronically undertaught — over-represent them.

## AP Precalculus
**Exam format:** 40 MCQ (2 hrs, 62.5%) + 4 FRQ (1 hr, 37.5%). Graphing calculator on designated parts.

**Skills needed:**
- Symbolic manipulation of polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, and trig expressions
- Build and interpret function models from scenarios and data sets
- Select an appropriate model type (linear/quadratic/exponential/log/sinusoidal) and justify the choice
- Evaluate trig functions via the unit circle; solve trig equations
- Transform functions (shifts, stretches, reflections, composition, inverses) and interpret parameter changes in context
- Perform regression and interpret residual behavior
- Work polar coordinates and parametric representations of motion

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–4):**
1. Polynomial and Rational Functions — rates of change, zeros, end behavior, asymptotes, holes
2. Exponential and Logarithmic Functions — properties, change of base, modeling growth/decay, semi-log plots
3. Trigonometric and Polar Functions — unit circle, sinusoidal modeling, identities, polar graphs
4. Functions Involving Parameters, Vectors, and Matrices — parametric motion, vector operations, matrix transformations (Unit 4 is assessed on the exam but emphasized less than Units 1–3)

**Question-bank notes:** Heavily scenario-based — every item should embed modeling in context. FRQ-style items require interpreting model parameters in plain language ("what does the 1.03 mean here?").

## AP Statistics
**Exam format:** 40 MCQ (90 min, 50%) + 6 FRQ (90 min, 50%) — the 6th FRQ is the multi-part Investigative Task.

**Skills needed:**
- Describe and compare distributions (shape, center, spread, outliers) in context
- Design samples and experiments; identify bias, confounding, and scope of inference (random sample → generalization; random assignment → causation)
- Reason about probability, including conditional probability and independence
- Select the correct inference procedure for a scenario (the single highest-value exam skill)
- Check and state conditions (random, 10%, normal/Large Counts) before procedures
- Carry out confidence intervals and significance tests; interpret p-values and interval meaning *in context, in plain language*
- Interpret computer output; connect slope, correlation, and residuals in regression
- On the Investigative Task: extend known methods to a novel scenario — the test of statistical thinking, not memorized procedures

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–9):**
1. Exploring One-Variable Data — displays, summary statistics, normal model, z-scores
2. Exploring Two-Variable Data — scatterplots, correlation, least-squares regression, residuals, two-way tables
3. Collecting Data — sampling methods, experimental design, blocking, scope of inference
4. Probability, Random Variables, and Probability Distributions — rules, expected value, binomial and geometric models
5. Sampling Distributions — sample proportions/means, Central Limit Theorem
6. Inference for Categorical Data: Proportions — one/two-proportion z-intervals and tests
7. Inference for Quantitative Data: Means — t-intervals and tests, paired vs. two-sample
8. Inference for Categorical Data: Chi-Square — goodness-of-fit, independence, homogeneity
9. Inference for Quantitative Data: Slopes — regression slope interval and test

**Question-bank notes:** Distractors = mis-selected procedures, condition violations, and classic interpretation errors ("95% probability the parameter is in this interval"). Every inference item should demand contextual interpretation, not just computation. Error types (Type I/II, power) are fertile MCQ ground.

## AP Computer Science A *(revised course effective 2025–26 / May 2026 exam)*
**Exam format:** 42 MCQ (55%) + 4 FRQ (45%). New FRQ alignment: Q1 Methods & Control Structures; Q2 Classes; Q3 Data Analysis with ArrayList; Q4 2D Array.[^1^]

**Skills needed:**
- Hand-trace code execution (loops, conditionals, method calls) — predict output and variable states
- Write complete Java methods and classes by hand: constructors, accessors/mutators, encapsulation
- Design algorithms: selection, iteration, nested control flow
- Traverse and manipulate arrays, ArrayLists, and 2D arrays; implement standard algorithms (find min/max, sum, search, frequency counts)
- Manipulate Strings (substring, indexOf, comparison)
- Read from text files and process real datasets using `File` and `Scanner` — **new required content for 2025–26**[^1^]
- Debug: identify logic errors, off-by-one errors, type errors

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–4, revised):**
1. Using Objects and Methods (15–25% of exam) — primitives vs. reference types, String/Math/wrapper classes, method calls
2. Selection and Iteration (25–35%) — boolean expressions, if/else, while/for loops, nested iteration, De Morgan's laws
3. Class Creation (10–18%) — class anatomy, encapsulation, constructors, static vs. instance, scope
4. Data Collections (30–40%) — arrays, ArrayList, 2D arrays, file I/O, dataset processing, standard algorithms

**Removed from required content:** the standalone inheritance/polymorphism unit (may still be taught as enrichment, but is not tested).[^1^]

**Question-bank notes:** MCQs are code-tracing exercises; distractors should encode execution errors (loop bound off by one, integer division, reference aliasing). FRQ practice must require writing full syntactically valid code — multiple-choice-only prep is a known failure mode for CSA.

## AP Computer Science Principles
**Exam format:** 70 MCQ (2 hrs, 70%) + Create Performance Task (through-course, 30%): program code, video, and written responses about program design, function, and purpose.

**Skills needed:**
- Design and implement a working program with input, output, sequencing, selection, iteration, and a student-developed procedure with parameters (Create task)
- Explain how an algorithm works and evaluate its efficiency (reasonable vs. unreasonable time)
- Apply abstraction: procedural and data abstraction
- Analyze data: extract insight, identify patterns, evaluate bias in data
- Evaluate the beneficial and harmful effects of computing innovations on society, economy, culture
- Explain how the internet works: protocols, packets, redundancy, fault tolerance
- Reason about computing tradeoffs: privacy, security, digital divide, crowdsourcing, legal/ethical concerns

**Knowledge needed (5 Big Ideas):**
1. Creative Development — collaboration, program design, development process, debugging
2. Data — binary representation, number systems, data compression (lossless/lossy), metadata, extracting information from data
3. Algorithms and Programming — variables, conditionals, loops, procedures, lists, simulation, algorithmic efficiency, undecidable problems
4. Computer Systems and Networks — internet structure, protocols (TCP/IP, DNS), fault tolerance, parallel/distributed computing
5. Impact of Computing — privacy, security (encryption, authentication), digital divide, crowdsourcing, intellectual property, ethics

**Question-bank notes:** The exam MCQs are scenario-based ("a company wants to... which approach?"), not code-heavy. Create-task prep needs rubric-aligned written-response practice — students lose points on *writing about* their code, not the code itself.

## AP Physics 1 *(revised effective 2025–26 / May 2025 exam onward)*
**Exam format:** 40 MCQ (80 min, 50%) + 4 FRQ (100 min, 50%). Four standardized FRQ types: Mathematical Routines, Translation Between Representations, Experimental Design, Qualitative/Quantitative Translation. Paragraph-length response eliminated; MCQ section delivered digitally via Bluebook, FRQs handwritten.[^2^]

**Skills needed:**
- Translate physical scenarios into models: choose relevant principles, define systems, draw free-body/energy/momentum representations
- Algebraic manipulation and derivation from first principles (no calculator-free shortcuts — relationships must be derived, not recalled)
- Translate between representations: verbal description ↔ diagram ↔ graph ↔ equation (now a dedicated FRQ type)
- Design experiments: identify variables, controls, procedures, and how data would support/refute a claim
- Analyze graphs: slope/area meaning, linearization of nonlinear relationships
- Proportional and ranking reasoning ("if mass doubles, what happens to...") — qualitative/quantitative translation
- Justify reasoning in concise sentences tied to physical principles

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–8, revised):**
1. Kinematics — 1D/2D motion, projectile motion, motion graphs
2. Force and Translational Dynamics — Newton's laws, friction, spring force
3. Work, Energy, and Power — conservation of energy, work-energy theorem
4. Linear Momentum — impulse, conservation, collisions
5. Torque and Rotational Dynamics — torque, rotational equilibrium, angular acceleration
6. Energy and Momentum of Rotating Systems — rotational kinetic energy, angular momentum
7. Oscillations — simple harmonic motion, springs, pendulums
8. Fluids — **moved from Physics 2 for 2025–26**: density, pressure, Pascal's and Archimedes' principles, continuity equation, Bernoulli's equation, Torricelli's theorem (~12–13% of exam)[^2^]

**Question-bank notes:** Every item should be scenario-first, equation-second. Distractors = misconception-driven (force implies motion, heavier falls faster, momentum not conserved in explosions). Include ranking tasks and "translation between representations" items — these are the new FRQ formats students won't have practiced elsewhere.

## AP Physics 2 *(revised effective 2025–26)*
**Exam format:** Identical structure to Physics 1: 40 MCQ + 4 FRQ of the same four types.[^2^]

**Skills needed:** Same skill set as Physics 1, plus:
- Multi-system reasoning (thermodynamic processes, circuits with multiple elements, multi-lens/mirror systems)
- Analyze energy transfers in thermal and electrical systems
- Interpret pressure-volume diagrams and thermodynamic processes
- Connect microscopic models to macroscopic observations (ideal gas, charge behavior)

**Knowledge needed (revised unit sequence):**
- Thermodynamics — temperature, ideal gas law, kinetic theory, first law, heat engines, entropy
- Electric Force, Field, and Potential — Coulomb's law, field/potential relationships
- Electric Circuits — Ohm's law, series/parallel, capacitors, RC circuits
- Magnetism and Electromagnetic Induction — forces on charges/currents, Faraday's and Lenz's laws
- Waves, Sound, and Physical Optics — **expanded emphasis for 2025–26**: standing waves, Doppler effect, interference, diffraction[^2^]
- Geometric Optics — **now its own unit**: reflection, refraction, lenses, mirrors, imaging[^2^]
- Modern Physics — photoelectric effect, atomic spectra, nuclear processes, blackbody radiation and Compton scattering now linked explicitly to thermodynamics[^2^]

(Fluids content moved to Physics 1 Unit 8.)[^2^]

**Question-bank notes:** Circuit analysis and ray diagrams are the most procedural content — generate systematic variants. Modern physics items are concept-heavy; test photon/energy-level reasoning, not formula recall.

## AP Physics C: Mechanics and AP Physics C: Electricity & Magnetism
**Exam format (each):** 35 MCQ (45 min, 50%) + 3 FRQ (45 min, 50%). Two separate half-year exams; calculus-based.

**Skills needed:**
- Derive relationships using calculus: differentiate/displace–velocity–acceleration chains, integrate for work/impulse with variable forces
- Set up and evaluate integrals over non-uniform distributions (mass, charge, current)
- Solve differential equations that arise physically (SHM, RC/LC circuits, velocity-dependent drag)
- Apply conservation laws in calculus form
- Translate between calculus expressions and physical meaning
- All Physics 1-style representation/experiment skills, at higher mathematical depth

**Knowledge needed — Mechanics:** kinematics (calculus form); Newton's laws; work, energy, power; systems of particles, momentum, collisions; rotation (moment of inertia, torque, angular momentum); oscillations; gravitation (orbits, energy)

**Knowledge needed — E&M:** electrostatics (Coulomb, field, potential); conductors and capacitors, dielectrics; Gauss's law applications; DC circuits (including RC transients); magnetic fields (Biot-Savart, Ampère's law); forces on moving charges and currents; electromagnetic induction (Faraday, Lenz, inductance, LC circuits); Maxwell's equations (conceptual level)

**Question-bank notes:** The discriminating skill is *setup* — writing the correct integral/differential equation before evaluating it. Items should reward setup and penalize formula-hunting. Distractors = common calculus slips (limits, sign conventions) plus physics misconceptions.

## AP Chemistry
**Exam format:** 60 MCQ (90 min, 50%) + 7 FRQ (105 min, 50%; 3 long, 4 short). Calculator allowed on FRQ only.

**Skills needed:**
- Quantitative problem-solving: stoichiometry, limiting reactants, solution concentration/dilution, gas law calculations
- Equilibrium calculations: K expressions, ICE tables, reaction quotient, Le Châtelier predictions
- Acid-base work: pH/pOH, Ka/Kb, buffers, titration curves (identify equivalence and half-equivalence points)
- Thermodynamics: ΔH, ΔS, ΔG relationships, Hess's law, calorimetry
- Kinetics: rate laws from data, integrated rate law graphs, half-life, activation energy from mechanisms
- Electrochemistry: cell potentials, standard reduction tables, Faraday's law, spontaneity
- Translate between representations: macroscopic observations ↔ particulate (particle-diagram) models ↔ symbolic equations — the signature AP Chem skill
- Design and analyze experiments: lab techniques, error analysis, spectroscopy data interpretation

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–9):**
1. Atomic Structure and Properties — electron configuration, periodic trends, photoelectron spectroscopy, Coulombic reasoning
2. Molecular and Ionic Compound Structure and Properties — bonding types, Lewis structures, VSEPR, hybridization, IMF intro
3. Intermolecular Forces and Properties — IMFs vs. properties, gases (ideal/nonideal), solutions, spectroscopy, mixtures/chromatography
4. Chemical Reactions — equation balancing, net ionic equations, reaction types, stoichiometry, titration intro
5. Kinetics — rates, rate laws, mechanisms, catalysts, collision theory
6. Thermodynamics — energy diagrams, enthalpy, entropy, Gibbs free energy
7. Equilibrium — K, Q, Le Châtelier, solubility equilibria (Ksp)
8. Acids and Bases — pH, strong/weak, Ka/Kb, buffers, titrations
9. Applications of Thermodynamics — entropy/free energy connections, galvanic and electrolytic cells, thermodynamic vs. kinetic control

**Question-bank notes:** Build two parallel tracks: quantitative items with procedural-error distractors (unit slips, stoichiometric ratio errors), and conceptual items with particulate-diagram stems. Titration curves and PES spectra are high-yield stimulus types. Hybrid 50:50 split is real — students fail both ways.

## AP Biology
**Exam format:** 60 MCQ (90 min, 50%) + 6 FRQ (90 min, 50%; 2 long, 4 short). Calculator allowed.

**Skills needed (CED Science Practices 1–6):**
- Explain biological concepts and processes in words, not just recall them
- Analyze visual representations: models, diagrams, flowcharts of pathways
- Design experiments and identify hypotheses, variables, controls, and predicted outcomes
- Represent and interpret data: construct and read graphs, evaluate trends and anomalies
- Perform statistical analysis: chi-square, standard error of the mean, 95% confidence intervals/error bars, rate calculations, Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, water potential, surface-area-to-volume
- Support claims with evidence and biological reasoning (claim-evidence-reasoning in FRQs)

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–8, organized by 4 Big Ideas — Evolution, Energetics, Information Storage & Transmission, Systems Interactions):**
1. Chemistry of Life — water properties, macromolecules, structure-function
2. Cell Structure and Function — organelles, membranes, transport, compartmentalization, origins
3. Cellular Energetics — enzymes, photosynthesis, cellular respiration, fitness
4. Cell Communication and Cell Cycle — signal transduction, mitosis, cell cycle regulation
5. Heredity — meiosis, Mendelian and non-Mendelian genetics, chromosomal inheritance
6. Gene Expression and Regulation — DNA/RNA structure, replication, transcription/translation, regulation, mutations, biotechnology
7. Natural Selection — evolution mechanisms, Hardy-Weinberg, speciation, phylogeny, origin of life
8. Ecology — responses to environment, energy flow, population ecology, community ecology, biodiversity, disruptions

**Question-bank notes:** Most exam stimuli are data or experiment descriptions — the corpus is tested *through* the practices. Items should pair a content tag (unit) with a practice tag (1–6) so coverage dashboards show both dimensions. Hardy-Weinberg and chi-square grid-ins are easy to parameterize and generate.

## AP Environmental Science
**Exam format:** 80 MCQ (90 min, 60%) + 3 FRQ (70 min, 40%): Q1 Design an investigation; Q2 Analyze an environmental problem and propose a solution; Q3 Analyze a problem with calculations.

**Skills needed:**
- Environmental math (calculator-free): population growth and doubling time (rule of 70), percent change, energy calculations, dimensional analysis
- Design field/lab investigations (independent/dependent variables, controls, sampling)
- Interpret maps, graphs, and data sets (climate data, population pyramids, resource consumption)
- Analyze environmental problems and *propose and evaluate* solutions with explicit tradeoffs (cost, feasibility, unintended consequences)
- Connect local phenomena to global systems (scale reasoning)

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–9):**
1. The Living World: Ecosystems — biomes, carbon/nitrogen/phosphorus/water cycles, primary productivity, trophic levels
2. The Living World: Biodiversity — ecosystem services, island biogeography, ecological tolerance, succession
3. Populations — survivorship curves, carrying capacity, population growth models, age structure diagrams, demographic transition
4. Earth Systems and Resources — plate tectonics, soil formation/composition, watersheds, solar radiation/seasons
5. Land and Water Use — agriculture (Green Revolution, irrigation, pesticides), mining, fishing, forestry, urbanization, sustainability
6. Energy Resources and Consumption — renewable/nonrenewable sources, energy use patterns, fuel types and impacts
7. Atmospheric Pollution — air pollutants, smog, acid deposition, indoor pollution, remediation
8. Aquatic and Terrestrial Pollution — water pollution, eutrophication, solid waste, sewage treatment, toxicology (LD50, dose-response)
9. Global Change — stratospheric ozone depletion, climate change causes/impacts, invasive species, endangered species, plus **key legislation and international agreements** (Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, CITES, Montreal Protocol, Kyoto, etc.)

**Question-bank notes:** Legislation and case studies are pure knowledge items; calculations are pure skill items — tag and balance both. FRQ 3 (calculations) is where strong-knowledge students bleed points; generate abundant parameterized variants.

# SECTION 3 — HISTORY & SOCIAL SCIENCES

**Shared exam architecture** (APUSH, World, Euro): 55 MCQ in sets of 3–4 per stimulus (55 min, 40%) + 3 SAQ (40 min, 20%) + 1 DBQ with 7 documents (60 min incl. 15-min reading, 25%) + 1 LEQ chosen from 3 (40 min, 15%). Administered digitally via Bluebook since 2025.[^3^]

**Shared skills needed (all three histories):**
- **Historical thinking skills:** identify/explain historical developments and processes; analyze sourcing and situation of primary and secondary sources (HIPP: historical situation, intended audience, purpose, point of view); analyze claims and evidence in arguments; contextualize; make connections across periods/regions; build evidence-based arguments
- **Reasoning processes:** comparison, causation, continuity and change over time (CCOT)
- **DBQ-specific:** evaluate-the-extent thesis construction; document grouping and analysis; sourcing for at least 2 documents; outside evidence integration; complexity (nuance, counterargument, multiple variables)
- **SAQ-specific:** concise, accurate identification/description/explanation in 2–4 sentences per part (no thesis required)
- **MCQ-specific:** reading stimuli for POV and argument; eliminating true-but-irrelevant options; periodization sense (placing undated events by internal evidence)

## AP United States History
**Knowledge needed (CED Periods 1–9, 1491–present):**
1. 1491–1607 — pre-contact Native societies, European exploration, Columbian Exchange, Spanish colonial system
2. 1607–1754 — colonial settlement patterns (British/French/Spanish/Dutch), labor systems, slavery's origins, colonial resistance
3. 1754–1800 — imperial conflicts, Revolution, Articles/Constitution, early republic politics
4. 1800–1848 — market revolution, Jacksonian democracy, reform movements, westward expansion, sectional tensions

7. 1890–1945 — imperialism, Progressivism, WWI, 1920s, Great Depression/New Deal, WWII
8. 1945–1980 — Cold War, civil rights movement, Great Society, Vietnam era, social movements
9. 1980–present — conservatism's rise, end of Cold War, globalization, technological and demographic change
- **Cross-cutting threads:** key legislation, major SCOTUS cases, social movements, migration/immigration patterns, foreign policy turning points

**Question-bank notes:** Distractors must be true-but-wrong-connection statements or plausible inversions — never absolutes. DBQ documents need genre variety (≥1 visual) and perspective diversity to make the HIPP point achievable. Rubrics must match the real 7-point DBQ and 6-point LEQ structures exactly.

8. Cold War and Decolonization (1900–present) — superpower rivalry, proxy conflicts, independence movements, nonalignment
9. Globalization (1900–present) — technology, economics, culture, environment, resistance to globalization

**Question-bank notes:** Cross-regional comparison items are the signature skill — stimuli should force comparison across regions within a period. DBQ focuses on 1450–2001; SAQ 1 uses secondary sources, SAQ 2 a primary source; LEQ choices span three time ranges (1200–1750, 1450–1900, 1750–2001).[^3^]

## AP African American Studies
**Exam format:** 60 MCQ (50%) + 4 FRQ (50%). Course draws on ~200 required sources across disciplines.

**Skills needed:**
- Analyze primary sources across media: texts, art, music, photography, data sets
- Apply interdisciplinary lenses (historical, literary, artistic, sociological) to the same material
- Connect developments across eras (continuity from African origins through contemporary movements)
- Evaluate sources for perspective, purpose, and context
- Build evidence-based written arguments using course sources

**Knowledge needed (CED Units 1–4):**
1. Origins of the African Diaspora (~900 BCE–16th century) — African geography and climate, early African societies and kingdoms (Egypt, Nubia, Aksum, Ghana, Mali, Songhai), trans-Saharan and Indian Ocean exchange, African traditions carried into the diaspora
2. Freedom, Enslavement, and Resistance (16th–19th centuries) — transatlantic slave trade, Middle Passage, slavery's development in the Americas, resistance and rebellion, abolition movements, Civil War and emancipation
3. The Practice of Freedom (1865–1940s) — Reconstruction, Jim Crow, Great Migration, Harlem Renaissance, Black institution-building (HBCUs, press, churches), early civil rights organizing
4. Movements and Debates (1940s–present) — civil rights movement, Black Power, Black feminism, debates over strategy and identity, contemporary movements and achievements in politics, culture, and scholarship

**Question-bank notes:** The required-source corpus is the knowledge gate — items should name and test specific sources (texts, artworks, speeches), not just general history. Interdisciplinary framing distinguishes this from APUSH coverage of the same events.

## AP United States Government and Politics
**Exam format:** 55 MCQ (80 min, 50%) + 4 FRQ (100 min, 50%): (1) Concept Application, (2) Quantitative Analysis, (3) SCOTUS Comparison, (4) Argument Essay.

**Skills needed:**
- Apply political concepts to hypothetical scenarios
- Analyze quantitative data: charts, tables, polling data, maps — identify trends and draw conclusions
- Compare a required SCOTUS case to a non-required case (facts, holding, reasoning, constitutional principle)
- Write an argument essay taking a position and supporting it with evidence from foundational documents

**Question-bank notes:** SCOTUS comparison items are uniquely generatable — pair a required case with a novel one. Argument essays must require document-based evidence. Data-analysis FRQ practice needs realistic charts/polls, not just text.

## AP Microeconomics
**Exam format:** Same structure: 60 MCQ (70 min, 66.7%) + 3 FRQ (60 min, 33.3%).

## AP English Language and Composition
**Exam format:** 45 MCQ (60 min, 45%) + 3 essays (2 hr 15 min, 55%): Synthesis, Rhetorical Analysis, Argument. MCQ answer choices reduced from 5 to 4 starting with the May 2025 exam.[^5^]

**Skills needed:**
- Rhetorical analysis: identify a writer's strategies (diction, syntax, imagery, appeals, structure) and explain how they achieve a purpose for an audience — the course's central skill
- Read dense nonfiction quickly and precisely (speeches, essays, journalism, letters)
- Write a synthesis essay: enter a conversation among 6–7 provided sources, integrating and citing at least 3 with commentary (not summary)
- Write an argument essay: take a defensible position, build a line of reasoning, deploy evidence, address complexity
- Revise and edit prose (MCQ writing questions: sentence combining, organization, precision)
- Analyze how evidence functions (MCQ reading questions: purpose of a detail, effect of a shift)

**Knowledge needed:**
- Rhetorical vocabulary: ethos/pathos/logos, tone, diction, syntax types (periodic, cumulative), schemes and tropes (parallelism, antithesis, anaphora, metaphor...)
- Argument anatomy: claims of fact/value/policy, evidence types, warrant, concession/refutation, logical fallacies
- Nonfiction genres and modes: narration, description, process analysis, comparison, classification, definition, exemplification, cause/effect
- Style and grammar mechanics relevant to revision questions
- No required reading canon — knowledge load is terminological, not textual

**Question-bank notes:** Stimuli must be real nonfiction passages with attribution. Essay rubrics follow the 6-point analytic model (Thesis 1 / Evidence & Commentary 4 / Sophistication 1). Commentary quality is the chronic failure point — feedback must target "explain HOW the evidence supports the claim," not just evidence presence.

## AP English Literature and Composition
**Exam format:** 55 MCQ (60 min, 45%) in 5 passage sets (≥2 prose fiction, ≥2 poetry) + 3 essays (2 hrs, 55%): Poetry Analysis, Prose Fiction Analysis, Literary Argument (student-selected work).[^6^]

- Close reading of unseen poetry and prose under time pressure
- Explain how literary devices (imagery, narration, structure, figurative language) produce meaning and complexity

# SECTION 8 — USING THIS DOCUMENT FOR A QUESTION BANK

1. **Classification → engine design.** Skill-dominant courses need procedural item generators (parameterized problems, worked step-level solutions, procedural-error distractors). Knowledge-dominant courses need stimulus-based items with misconception distractors and corpus-coverage tagging. Hybrids need both, balanced to each exam's actual blueprint.
2. **Skills list → question-type map.** Every skill above should map to at least one item type. If a skill has no item type, it's a coverage hole (e.g., most APUSH tools never test periodization; most stats tools never test experimental design).
3. **Knowledge list → tagging taxonomy.** Use CED unit names as the top-level tags exactly as written here. This enables coverage dashboards ("Period 5: 40 questions; Period 2: 0") and spaced retrieval by unit.
4. **Rubric fidelity is non-negotiable.** DBQ = 7 points (Thesis 1 / Contextualization 1 / Evidence 3 / Analysis & Reasoning 2). LEQ = 6 points. SAQ = 1 point per part, no thesis. English essays = 6-point analytic rubrics. Science FRQs award method steps, not just answers. A tool that teaches the wrong scoring game actively harms students.
5. **Watch the revision cycle.** Courses redesigned in the last three years: Psychology (2024–25), Physics 1 & 2 (2024–25/2025–26), Computer Science A (2025–26), Latin (2025–26), English MCQ format (2024–25). New launches: Business with Personal Finance and Cybersecurity (2026–27), AP Networking (planned 2027–28). Re-verify formats each summer when College Board updates CEDs.
